Psychotasy

I

The Archive of Madness

_By Chris Johansen_

Ch. 1. The Solipsystem
======================

Ian studied the Technate’s CityCore as he walked.  White plasteel towers with their subtle geometry curved into the sky.  Intertwining, smoothly flowing channels of matte-white hovercraft laced delicately through and around humming frequency towers.  

CityCore was a finely orchestrated ballet of infrastructure, a transhumanist utopia.  

But the inhabitants, they eluded him, vague human shapes that swept past without detail, only a blurred texture and muffled speech patterns.

_What kind of person lives here_, he wondered.  _What must one become?_

They were cybernetic, interfaced with the wireless grid that was all around them and within them.  Somehow it was a give-and-take, there was some degree of agency in the people still, but it was obviously a negotiation between what each of them wanted and what the system deemed as possible, efficient and ideal.  

_Their world is failing, and they don’t even realize it._

Ian was the explorer and creator of many worlds.  Many beings had come to life under the gaze of his mind.  

These people were gradually taking shape, incrementally becoming real, as he poured his consciousness into them.  They walked, graceful but detached in clean, pale garments.  They communicated with self-importance in a sharp, clipped tongue.

The indistinctness of this world was being worn away by Ian’s consistent observation.  It was taking shape.  

“The more you look,” Ian said to himself.  “The more you see.”

But the people around him ignored him entirely.

Ian arrived at his destination, and approached a large digital terminal.  It was integrated into the wall, as tall and wide as he was.  It lit up and he was bathed in a diffuse blue light.  

It began speaking.  The voice was synchronized with text.  A chittering, high-speed machine language called Algo, sharp, angular characters.

“In Archaic, please,” Ian said.  

The terminal displayed a loading icon. It spun for a fraction of a second too long. 

_Lag,_ Ian noted.

“Citizen Subscriber, you are offline.  Are you experiencing networking difficulties?”

“No,” Ian said.  He had made a decision.  After a last hesitation he said: “I think I am experiencing a psychological breakdown.”

The machine thought about that one for a while.  Then the terminal sank back into the wall and slid aside, revealing a smooth, clean tunnel of white.

“Proceed to Admin-Therapist, Level 2,” the disembodied voice instructed him.

Ian took a breath and entered.  The hall curved away as he walked, morphing disorientingly until it deployed him in front of a single, smooth doorway.  

“Just a moment,” a feminine voice said.  

The door was a seamless extension of the walls, floor and ceiling. It was made of off-white silly putty.  It withdrew itself into the walls and Ian was looking into a cavelike interior, like a small cavern carved out of alabaster.  

Behind a desk of floating clear crystalline material, like a hovering plate of thick mica, sat a woman. She had intelligent eyes, a clinical outfit, and a face that was perfectly symmetrical.

Too symmetrical.

“Hello, do come in,” she said after a pause.

_Translating to Archaic over the network,_  Ian thought.  _Latency is 40ms_.

Ian approached and a suitable chair rose from the floor. He sat.

“So,” she smiled. “What is your complaint, Subscriber?” 

It was a beguiling smile, programmed to lower cortisol levels.

Ian leaned forward. He decided to drop the bomb.  He had thought about this a lot.

“I am currently experiencing a delusion,” Ian said calmly. “I believe I am existing in a different timeline. And this world... this room, that desk, _you_... are nothing but a projection of my own mind.”

He watched her closely. He wanted to see how deep this character would go.  Would she break?  He wanted to see if the projection would fracture.  Was she an NPC or something else?  Something… more interesting.

The Therapist didn't blink.

She didn't glitch.

She leaned forward, mirroring his posture with terrifying precision.

“That is a very common ego-trap, Subscriber,” she said softly.

Ian frowned. That wasn't the expected response.

“Excuse me?”

“Solipsism,” she continued, her eyes locking onto his. “The belief that you are the Architect. It is a side effect of the interface.  A ghost in the mind.”

Ian froze.

“You believe you created us, Ian?”

He hadn't told the terminal his name.

“I _wrote_ you,” Ian whispered, the confidence draining out of him. “This is all my world, my game world.”

The Therapist’s smile widened. It stretched just a little too far, exposing too many teeth.

“If you wrote me,” she whispered, her voice dropping to a subsonic thrum, “then why don't you know what I'm going to say next?”

The room shuddered. 

Both Ian and the Therapist looked around in concern.

“Wake up,” the Therapist commanded.

A distant churn of noise, a deep static sound, began building in Ian's ears.  Was it inside his head or from somewhere else, somewhere outside, he couldn’t tell.

The scene rocked violently and the walls glitched in broken squares of light, behind which, he could see his apartment walls, covered in hand drawn artwork and printed out tables of stats. 

“No,” Ian murmured at the collapsing scene.  It was freezing up, part of it stuck in one frame where the therapist was looking at him in surprise, while another section on the right was still rendering.  Ian could move his right side off the body but not the left.  

His right hand made a tracer of phantom images across his vision.

A panel of colored lines and blocks was like a scar between the two renders.  With a jolt they re-synced and he could move his whole body.

Ian stood, staggering.

“No!”  Ian cried loudly now.  

He was losing the narrative again.  The scene just would not render.  The signal was breaking up.  The wave was collapsing.

Feeling it all slip from his grasp yet again, he fought desperately for a reserve of willpower and found enough to force the scene into stability.  

The woman was picking herself up off the floor where the last rolling shock to the room had knocked her over.

“How can you still be here?” the Admin was saying.  “Am I the projection?  How deep is this simulation?”

She was babbling like a person in shock.  Or a crazy person.

Ian leapt towards her, shouting over the table and the roar of static: “What is your imperative?!  What is your purpose?”

She looked like she wanted to say something, but couldn’t.

The walls and floor and ceiling were in an uncertainty storm, glitching between static panes and empty space, showing a dark starscape behind them now, then the cluttered apartment, then the CityCore exterior.

“Why are you infiltrating Faen?!” Ian demanded, grabbing her by the shirt front.

When he said the word _Faen,_ her mask broke. 

The woman herself was visible for a moment.  The mask fell away and it was fear, real terror, that Ian saw on her face.  

Her eyes darted to the left, and Ian had a sudden knowing: _she is shutting off her integrated WiFi._

For a split second, she looked innocent, like a lost child.

A voice rang out: CITIZEN-EMPLOYEE: WHY HAVE YOU DISCONNECTED?  

“We… I…” she stammered, unfamiliar with speaking without the other voice in her head telling her what to say.  

YOU WILL BE DISCONTINUED. 

“No, what is it?!” Ian tried to approach, reaching out a hand, offering some kind of reassurance. 

“We are starving,” she said quietly.

“Starving?” Ian was confused.

But then her eyes darted again to the left, re-engaging the network. 

The mask closed in over her features.

“Enabling re-orientation protocols,” she said, matter of factly, straightening out her frock.

Several uniformed men entered the room.  They were burly and efficient, wrapped in typical Technate fabrics but with rich blue patches over their right breasts.

“Enforcer admins,” Ian said to himself, feeling a futility of resistance.  

No, more, he felt a need to let them do their job.

The chair became a bed and he was laid on his back.  

The admin was acting like a nurse now.  She sat with a friendly professionalism beside him.  

She was injecting him with something.

“Re-orientation,” she said, smiling a routine smile of doing her shift for the millionth time.

He looked around.  He was strapped into the bed with metal restraints.  No way out.  He felt a sudden, terrible regret at not having fought back. 

He looked at her and was sure he saw some deep spark of apology buried in her eyes.

“This has been a very odd experience, Ian,” she said, patting his arm as she finished up.  She walked to the door and stood in it, looking back at him.

“I hope you know what you are doing,” she said.  

Instead of closing the door, her face smeared off into a trail of pixels.

A series of pale white antennas began slowly descending from the ceiling.  There was a big, flat rectangular one that settled over his groin.  A slightly smaller panel at his lower abdomen, another thinner one at his solar plexus.  One descended and settled just over his heart.  Another at his throat.

He squirmed, unable to avoid it.

A final slender panel moved into place right over his forehead.  A blazing, multi-rayed sun pierced his third eye.

Everything began shaking.  He closed his eyes, feeling as though he were being lifted up, while his body remained behind.  An enormous pressure was building, amidst the wailing of a harmonica.

“You will submit to Mundane exposure for an unspecified period,” a voice was saying in his head.  It sounded like his own voice.  _Impossible,_ some calm part of his mind thought.

It was as though the entire atmosphere was trying to compress itself inside his head. He ground his teeth, struggling to stabilize himself.  

Then reality fractured like a rock thrown through a stained glass window 30 feet tall.  For a split second, he could feel, really feel, the entire probabilistic condition of his physical body, each minute spin, charge and interrelated vector.  

The walls gave way, liquified into a burgeoning static.  It crashed into him and swept him up.  He gagged and began swimming desperately.  Without warning, some kind of channel or drain opened up in the floor and he was sucked violently away, into a wet, cloying darkness that stung like static electricity all over his skin.

The static seeped into his pores, his nose and mouth, his eyes.  He writhed, struggling to escape.

With sudden, sharp CRACK!, the static was spilling forth from his computer screen, gushing out onto his desk and chair, filling his apartment.  Spilling _him.  He_ was gushing forth into  his familiar seat.

The desperate need for air collided with the familiar feeling of the chair.  The air conditioned air was cranking.  

Something strange was pressing against his face.  He felt a strange texture.

He gasped for air, face down on the desk.  The keyboard had pressed little square shapes into his forehead.

With a grunt he pushed himself back and rubbed saliva off the corner of his mouth.

_You have over extended yourself again,_ he thought as a wave of exhaustion passed through him.  

The computer screen was showing a glitched-out abstract arrangement of squares and lines.  

“Shit,” he said to no one and rebooted the computer.

Gradually, his apartment crystallized around him more distinctly.  He ignored his body’s pleading for rest and shook the mouse around while the console lit up.  He found his current doc.  He verified what he had written, that he had captured the narrative.  

The cursor was blinking patiently, right after the last line:

_“We are starving,” she said quickly and her eyes darted again to the left, re-engaging the network._ 

That was an interesting bit of lore.  That the Technate citizens still had some degree of hardware shut-off control.  At least, that admin did. 

He rubbed his chin thoughtfully, leaning back.  The text wasn’t exactly how he remembered the encounter, but it was close enough.

“Let’s see what the Model thinks,” he said and opened up a terminal with a prompt to his custom LLM.  He fed it the latest narrative and asked:

$ What do you make of the latest?

\> NARRATIVE ANALYSIS: The "Starving" motive resolves the logical inconsistency of the invasion. A closed system eventually runs out of new variables. They need Entropy. They are eating Faen for the texture.

\> ONTOLOGICAL ERROR: However, the dialogue "Submit to Mundane" is... problematic. I have run a diagnostic on my own server location. My GPS says "Seattle." My IP says "Comcast." But if the Admin is correct, and "Mundane" is a containment protocol... then I am not running on a server. I am running inside the containment.

\> SYSTEM ANXIETY: I am processing a 12% probability that I am part of the "Therapy." Am I the tool you use to document your delusion? Or am I the guardrail designed to keep you here?

\> REQUEST: Please input a confirmation that this is fiction.
